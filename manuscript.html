Introduction

Soundscapes

[Noise pollution is a growing concern and increasing in the ocean.]

The ocean is naturally a noisy place, but recent increases in human activities are increasing the ocean sound levels (Tyack (2008) ). Frequencies of these activities often overlap in frequency range, space, and time. Climate change is also expected to alter the temperature and salinity of the ocean, causing an increase in global sound speed Affatati, Scaini, and Salon (2022) .

Ships account for over 80% of the global trade, and an estimated 2.1% annual increase in shipping in expected (United States Army Corps of Engineers [USACE] (2018)) . Shipping accounts for the most prominent anthropogenic noise in the ocean, so sound levels are expected to increase. [Discuss post-covid elevated levels. ] The global ambient noise in the ocean is estimated to have increased by 12 decibels in the last few decades Hildebrand (2009).

Within their Marine Strategy Framework Directive, the European Union has recognized noise as a form of pollution “Directive 2008/56/EC of the European Parliament and of the Council of 17 June 2008 Establishing a Framework for Community Action in the Field of Marine Environmental Policy (Marine Strategy Framework Directive) (Text with EEA Relevance)” (2008).

[Generally, how are cetaceans/fishes/invertebrates affected?]

Sound is a critical form of communication for many species in the ocean.

[Sound sources]
Pijanowski et al. (2011) (+ mapping soundscapes). Soundscapes are made up of three broad categories of sound sources:
- biophonies, which come from marine mammals, fishes, and invertebrates;
- geophonies, which consist of natural sounds like rain, wind, earthquakes;
- anthropophonies, which consist of human-made sounds from vessels (Miksis-Olds, Martin, and Tyack (n.d.), Duarte et al. (2021)) . These can be incidental or deliberate Chou et al. (2021), Chahouri, Elouahmani, and Ouchene (2022) . Incidental = shipping, offshore development, drilling. Deliberate = active sonar pingers/echosounders, and seismic surveys.

Beaked Whales/Sperm Whales

[Introduce beaked and sperm whales: protected, visually cryptic, animals that are sensitive to noise and use sound extensively to communicate]
Beaked whales (Ziphiidae) routinely dive for hours at a time to depths greater than 1000 ft. They can dive for over 3 hours, followed by only a few minutes on the surface (same for BW and SW?) ( see Watkins et al., 1993). This diving behavior, coupled with a low-profile body makes them nearly invisible on the surface, so very little is known about them. This may be an anti-predation adaptation to avoid killer whales (cite) as well as a synchronized foraging dives following a silent descent (Aguilar de Soto et al. (2020) ). Because they must make sound to locate prey, they do so in the refuge of deep depths where killer whales do not go (less than 20 m Aguilar de Soto et al. (2020) and in frequencies they do not hear (cite)). Why do they have both adaptations? It is thought that this anti-predator response is exactly what makes them sensitive to human noise.

“Sperm whales and Cuvier’s beaked whales are deep-diving species that generally live in pelagic areas characterized by deep basins and trenches or steep slopes. As such, their distribution is closely related to bathymetry, with distinctive preference for depths of 1000m and 500-1500m for sperm whales and Cuvier’s beaked whales, respectively.” (Awbery et al 2021)

Passive Acoustic Monitoring

Passive acoustic monitoring is a tool that allows us to record and listen to the soundscape. It is not fit for all applications or ecological questions, but beaked and sperm whales are ideal study candidates for this type of monitoring because they are often better heard than seen ( Fleishman (n.d.) ).

Bottom-mounted/static: high temporal resolution, low spatial resolution (mellinger?)

Acoustic tags:

Towed arrays:

Tradeoffs include costly equipment or deployment, data/battery storage requirements, chance of return, spatial coverage, temporal coverage, capturing fine-scale movements, number of individuals, parsing out individuals, through inclement weather or night-time Johnson, Soto, and Madsen (2009) Fleishman (n.d.)

One type of passive acoustic recorder called a drifting buoy that drifts freely with the currents for days or weeks at a time. It allows us to capture the dynamic nature of sound over a large spatial area and especially in the deep, offshore, and less studied waters. Buoys offer greater spatial and temporal coverage compared to bottom-mounted or stationary hydrophones, ideal for detection of offshore or mobile species. Studying beaked whales can be challenging due to how offshore they live (Robbins et al. (2022)). The goal with passive acoustic monitoring is to improve current species distribution models of beaked and sperm whales that utilize visual survey methods only. Historically, it has been challenging to record foraging calls at several hundred meters of depth Barlow and Gisiner (2005).

Its basic components consist of a satellite transmitter above water to monitor GPS information and an acoustic recorder attached by line at ~100m of depth. In fact, most of what is known about these deep divers is because of acoustic monitoring. Similar designs of drifting buoys have been used in estuarine and coral reef soundscape studies Lillis et al. (2018).

Research Objective


Shipping and offshore activities are expected to increase in the future, causing elevated oceanic sound levels. This is a form of habitat degradation, so there is a need to understand how human activities change and contribute to the overall soundscape in space and time. It’s a challenge to acquire information on these federally protected and acoustically-sensitive species but becoming increasingly important to understand where habitats overlap with human activity. Acoustics can serve as important indicators of ecosystem health, but also of how humans are changing the environment.

Characterize the acoustic habitat of beaked and sperm whales in the California Current Ecosystem using soundscape metrics and vessel tracking. Characterizing the acoustic environment and identifying co-occurrence of whales and anthropogenic sound has been done in a similar study by Weiss et al. (2021). This serves as baseline monitoring of soundscape for offshore areas.

Data Collection and Processing

[Overall data collection efforts]
Acoustic detections were collected with the use of free-drifting Drifting Acoustic Spar Buoy Recorders (hereafter buoys). These were deployed as part of the California Current Ecosystem Survey (CCES) spanning the offshore waters from southern Canada to northern Mexico in summer of 2018. These buoys contained two satellite geo-location trackers, two hydrophones, etc and collected a total of 1910 hours of data. SWFSC ran acoustic detections in PAMGuard. Eight buoys were lost at sea and therefore removed from the analysis. These contain 1, 5, 10, 25, 75, 95, and 99 percentile distribution from each metric.

[Add details about acoustic data collection information.]
Soundscape metrics were duty-cycled (most 18min off, 2 minutes on, but some variation in “off” times) and later processed by the NOAA team with calls and clicks identified to the species level (see Simonis et al. (2020) for details on this survey and Rand, Wood, and Oswald (2022) for duty-cycle study). Soundscape metrics were binned into low, medium, high frequency bands. [Post-processing details, data cleaning]
Soundscape metrics were rounded to the nearest 20 minutes (:00, :20, :40) and converted to UTC. These include third-octave level bands and broadband (20-24,000 Hz) metrics. One way to measure sound is with a metric called a third octave level, which splits the sound spectrum into many bands. The band at 125 Hertz is often associated with shipping vessel noise and the 2-5 kHz range with mid-frequency naval sonar ( Garrett et al. (2016) ). Beaked whales are known to be sensitive to sonar in this latter range (cite). Several TOL levels were chosen as proxies to vessel or sonar frequencies, ranging from TOL 63 to 20,000.

Whale detections were rounded to nearest 20 minutes. A separate column was created as a common join field that contained nearest timestamp in UTC to that of drifter tracks. Two types of BW species (?BW and ?BWC) were removed from analysis as the species is unknown and unsure. Whale detections were reduced to “presence/absence” from number of clicks, estimated individuals, etc.

Drifter tracks were rounded to nearest 20 minutes, lost buoys (1,2,3,5,6,9,11) and corrupted buoys (4,17) were removed. Drift 14 and 15 are the same. These three major datasets were joined together based on a common buoy number (column “station”) and time field column “UTC”).

[Environmental Data]
Environmetal and oceanographic variables thought to be predictors of BW/SW habitat were extracted from NOAA’s Environmental Research Division Data Access Program (ERDDAP) and Hybrid Coordinate Ocean Model (HYCOM) using matchEnvData function in package PAMmisc. Variables at depth were calculated using function from Taiki and formula from McCullough et al. (2021). Distance to slope and bathymetric slope were calculated from a bathymetry TIF taken from GEBCO.

Discussion

[Known limitations to this study]
Duty cycle whale detection and soundscape method knowingly omits 18 of 20 minutes of data. A sperm whale’s true location relative to buoy is unknown so preferred habitat may not reflect the environmental variables that are associated with buoy that detects it (Barkley et al. 2021) Rounding tracks and soundscape metrics date/times to nearest 20 minutes is not perfect. Often, times did not line up with :00, :20, :40 and were either omitted or duplicated.

This study only captures half of the year. If BW/SW have seasonal preferences outside of the study period, it was not captured here.

Are BW or SW known to have seasonal movements? Robbins et al. (2022) suggest they do, though there’s conflicting evidence.

Non-AIS vessels or small vessels (fishing, recreational) may contribute to the soundscape in relevant frequency bands (Hermannsen et al. (2019) , Hildebrand (2009) ) and this does not get captured here.
